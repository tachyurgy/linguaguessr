01

The framing — and the one tension to manage

The whole strategy rests on a persona choice. Get this right and the rest writes itself.

LinguaGuessr currently wears a B2B suit: "a demo by Levelbrook, a senior software consulting practice," with a Hire us button. That funnel sells five-figure engagements to companies. A Patreon sells $3–$35/mo affection to individual players. Those are two different audiences with two different wallets — and a consulting firm begging for coffee money looks desperate, while an indie maker who happens to also freelance looks normal.

So you run two voices on one site, cleanly separated:

For companies

"Hire us"

Stays exactly as-is. Professional, Levelbrook-branded, points at consulting.levelbrook.com. This is the real money.

For players

"Keep me building" indie voice

First-person, anonymous, warm. "I make weird little games — chip in if you liked this." Coffee, Patreon, Bitcoin.

The mental model: the player never needs to know the two are the same person. To a company, you're a practice. To a player, you're "the one person who made this." Both are true. The anonymity rule (no real name anywhere) actually helps here — the indie persona is just "the maker."

The one-line pitch (memorize this)

Core pitch

"I make small, strange, lovingly-built browser games — no ads, no trackers, no investors. If this one made your day a little better, you can keep me fueled and chasing the next."

Every other piece of copy is a remix of that sentence. It does three jobs at once: establishes scarcity (one person, hand-made), names the values (no ads/tracking/VC — the things players hate), and makes a small, guilt-free ask (a coffee, not a salary).

02

Voice & positioning

People don't fund software. They fund a person with a mission they want to see continue.

The thing patrons are actually buying is the feeling of "I'm keeping something good alive." Your job in the copy is to make the mission legible and the maker human — without ever whining.

Do

  • Be specific about where money goes ("more clips, more languages, more games").
  • Lead with the gift you already gave them (a free game they enjoyed), then ask.
  • Sound like a maker on a roll, not a creator in crisis.
  • Name the enemy: ads, tracking, dark patterns, VC enshittification.
  • Keep the ask tiny and reversible ("a coffee," "cancel anytime").

Don't

  • Guilt-trip ("if you don't support me this dies"). Reads as manipulation.
  • Mention rent, bills, or hardship. Pity converts worse than momentum.
  • Use corporate/consulting language ("solutions," "engagements") in the player ask.
  • Over-promise rewards you can't sustain (daily videos, custom games).
  • Beg on every screen. One warm ask at the right moment beats ten nags.

The micro-story (use on the Patreon page + About)

~80-word origin

"LinguaGuessr started as a weekend experiment: could you guess a language just by ear? It turned into a machine-verified corpus of real speech from 40+ languages, a daily challenge, and a tiny obsession. I build things like this because the web used to be full of weird, free, hand-made stuff — and I want to keep making some. Patreon is how I buy myself the time to do it."

The money stack — three rails, three jobs

Don't think of these as competitors. Each one catches a different kind of person at a different moment.

☕
Impulse · one-time

Buy Me a Coffee

The frictionless "that was fun, here's $5" tap. No commitment, no account for them. Catches the 95% who'll never subscribe to anything but will toss a coin. Your highest-volume rail.

🎟️
Loyalty · recurring

Patreon

The 2–3% who love it enough to fund it monthly. Small in count, but this is the only predictable income — the number that lets you say "I can afford to keep building." The strategic core.

₿
Niche · crypto

Bitcoin tip jar

A small but real slice of a tech/HN/Reddit audience prefers crypto and will tip more, anonymously, when it's the path of least resistance. Near-zero effort to add. Pure upside.

Why all three, not just one: Buy Me a Coffee also does monthly memberships, so people ask "why both?" Answer: Patreon is the brand creators recognize and trust for recurring support, has better tiers/community tooling, and is where you build a public "X patrons" number that becomes social proof. BMC is the low-friction one-time catcher. Use BMC for coffees, Patreon for membership. Don't split memberships across both.

The Patreon tier ladder

Four tiers. Priced on a psychological ladder, not a cost ladder. The middle one is the target.

☕Espresso$3/mo You keep the lights on. Name in the credits + the dev log where I post what I'm building next.
🗺️Cartographer$7/mo Everything above — plus a vote on which languages & modes come next, and work-in-progress peeks first.
★ most popular — design everything to point here 🎧Polyglot$15/mo Everything above — plus early access to every new game I ship, and I'll genuinely chase down any language or clip you want in the corpus.
💎Patron$35/mo Everything above — your name on the supporters wall, a real thank-you, and a direct line to request a feature and have it actually happen.

Exactly what you promise at each tier

Tiers are cumulative — each one is "everything below, plus one new thing." Every promise here is deliberately chosen to cost you ≈nothing per extra patron (or to scale), so 5 patrons and 500 patrons are the same workload. Read the "costs you" line on each — that's the part that protects you.

☕

Espresso — $3/mo

"thank you + a seat at the table"

You promise:

  • Their name in the in-game credits / supporters list (refreshed monthly).
  • Access to the patron-only dev log — 1–2 short posts a month: what shipped, what's next, a screenshot.
  • A patron badge/shout-out if you ever add comments or a Discord.

Costs you: one name in a list + the posts you'd want to write anyway. Zero per-patron time.

🗺️

Cartographer — $7/mo

"a say in where it goes"

Everything in Espresso, plus you promise:

  • A vote in monthly polls — next language, next mode, next game idea.
  • Work-in-progress previews: screenshots and early builds posted to patrons before they go public.

Costs you: a 5-minute poll + posting a screenshot you already have. Scales to any patron count.

🎧

Polyglot — $15/mo

★ target tier

Everything above, plus you promise:

  • Early access to every new game / mode you ship — roughly a week before the public.
  • "I'll chase your request": suggest a language or clip and you'll genuinely try to get it into the corpus. Word it as best-effort — "I'll try," never "I will."

Costs you: share a link a week early (free) + best-effort requests you were sourcing anyway.

💎

Patron — $35/mo

"on the wall + a real line to me"

Everything above, plus you promise:

  • Their name on a permanent supporters wall on the site (not just monthly credits).
  • A personal thank-you note when they join.
  • One feature request taken seriously per quarter — if it's feasible, you'll actually build it.

Costs you: real time — but few patrons reach this tier, and the cap ("one per quarter, if feasible") keeps it bounded.

The four rules of what you promise

  1. Only promise what costs ≈0 per patron, or scales. Credits, dev logs, polls, early links — all of these are the same effort whether 5 or 5,000 people pay.
  2. Word every time-based promise as best-effort. "I'll try to add your language," not "I will." A guarantee you can't keep churns patrons faster than no promise at all.
  3. Cap the one expensive promise. The $35 feature request is bounded to "one per quarter, if feasible." That's the only tier that touches your time, and it's deliberately rate-limited.
  4. Under-promise, over-deliver. The dev log is the real retention driver — show up twice a month and patrons stay. Surprise extras (a shout-out, a clip someone asked for) buy more goodwill than any tier perk.

Never promise these — they scale linearly with patron count and will make you resent the whole thing: custom/personal games, scheduled 1:1 calls, physical merch, daily content, anything "exclusive" that paywalls part of the actual game, or hard guarantees ("every requested language will be added"). Every one of these is a trap that turns a hobby into an unpaid obligation.

The pricing psychology

  • $3 floor, not $1. A dollar tier attracts the highest-maintenance patrons for the least money. $3 filters for people who actually value it and barely feel it.
  • $15 is the anchor target. Make it visibly "most popular," give it the best perceived value jump, and let $35 exist mainly to make $15 look reasonable (the classic decoy — most "Patron" buyers were going to be your superfans anyway).
  • Each tier = "everything above + one new thing." Cumulative tiers feel generous and are easier to deliver than bespoke per-tier rewards.
  • Annual option: turn on Patreon's annual billing (≈2 months free). Locks in cash and cuts churn.

Trap to avoid: don't paywall the game. LinguaGuessr stays 100% free forever. Patrons fund the future (new games, new languages, your time) and get nice-to-haves (votes, early access, credit) — never access to the thing that's already public. Paywalling a free game kills the goodwill that drives the donations.

What you actually owe patrons (keep it sustainable)

The #1 way creators burn out is over-promising rewards. Design rewards that cost you ~nothing to deliver.

RewardReal effortVerdict
Name in credits / supporters wallOne line in a list, updated occasionallyFree. Always include.
Dev log posts1–2 short posts/month — what you shipped, what's next, a screenshotCheap. This is the product.
Vote on next language/modeA Patreon poll, 5 minFree + makes them feel ownership.
Early accessShare a link a week earlyEasy, but only if you ship regularly.
"Chase your requested clip/language"Best-effort, not a guarantee — word it that wayFine if framed as "I'll try."
Custom games / 1:1 calls / merchHours per patron, doesn't scaleAvoid. This is how you start hating it.

The honest truth about reward delivery: the single highest-retention "reward" is simply posting a dev log twice a month so patrons see the thing is alive and they're part of it. If you do nothing else, do that. Patrons churn when it goes quiet, not when rewards are thin.

06

On-site placement — where & when to ask

Timing beats prominence. Ask right after delight, never before it.

  1. End-of-game Summary screen — primary ask. They just finished five rounds and (ideally) had fun. A compact "Enjoying LinguaGuessr? It's made by one person — buy me a coffee or ride along on Patreon → Support" strip. This is the warm moment; this is where conversions happen.
  2. Menu footer — persistent, low-key. A "☕ Support" link next to "How to play / About." Always reachable, never in the way.
  3. The Support modal — the full pitch. Opens from either of the above. Holds: the one-line pitch, the ☕ Buy-Me-a-Coffee button, the Patreon tier ladder, and the ₿ Bitcoin address with copy-to-clipboard. (Already built in src/support.jsx — pending your sign-off on this strategy.)
  4. Daily Challenge done state — soft moment. After a streak milestone (e.g. 7-day), a one-time "you've played a week straight — want to keep me going?" nudge. Rate-limit hard; once is charming, every day is a nag.

Do not put a donate button in the top nav next to "Hire us," and do not interrupt a round with an ask. Mid-game monetization is the fastest way to make a free game feel cheap.

07

Launch & growth playbook

A Patreon with 0 patrons and no posts converts nobody. Seed it before you point traffic at it.

Before you announce

  • Create the Patreon, write the about page (use the §02 micro-story), set up all four tiers, and publish 2–3 dev-log posts first so it doesn't look abandoned on day one.
  • Turn on annual billing. Add a goal ("at $X/mo I can spend a full day a week on this").
  • Wire the site (Support modal + Summary strip). Put the real BMC/Patreon/BTC values into src/support.jsx.

The traffic you already have

  • Hacker News / Reddit relaunch: a "Show HN: I added X new languages to LinguaGuessr" type post drives a spike — and the Summary-screen ask catches that spike. The support layer is what monetizes traffic you were getting for free anyway.
  • r/geoguessr, r/languagelearning, r/linguistics, r/webgames are warm audiences. Lead with the game, not the Patreon.
  • Dev-log cadence is the growth engine: every "added 5 languages / new Accent Mode" post is a fresh reason to share and a fresh conversion moment.

The flywheel: ship something small → post a dev log → share it → traffic spikes → Summary-screen ask converts a few coffees + a patron or two → that funds the next small thing. Keep the loop turning; the absolute numbers compound slowly but the habit is the asset.

08

Realistic numbers (so you're not disappointed)

Set expectations honestly. This is a tip jar that grows, not a salary on day one.

  • Conversion is low and that's normal. Free-game → paying-supporter runs well under 1% of players for one-time tips, and a fraction of that for recurring. A viral spike of 20k players might yield a few dozen coffees and a handful of patrons.
  • Patreon income is small but compounding. 10 patrons averaging ~$8 = ~$80/mo. Unglamorous — but it's recurring, it's proof people value the work, and it grows every time you ship + post.
  • The real ROI is optionality + signal. Even a modest "47 people fund this" number is social proof on the consulting side ("ships products people pay to support"), and the dev-log habit makes you a better, more visible builder.
  • Fees: Patreon takes ~8–12% all-in; BMC ~5%; Bitcoin ~network fee only. Don't optimize fees at this scale — optimize for the path of least friction for the supporter.
09

Bitcoin in 3 simple ways

You asked how to get a wallet + an address you can paste on the site. Here are three, easiest → most secure. For a public tip jar, Method 2 is the sweet spot.

Method 1 — A custodial app (fastest, cash-out friendly) 5 min

Cash App or Strike (or Coinbase). Sign up, verify ID once, and the app gives you a Bitcoin receive address instantly. They hold the keys for you and you can sell straight to your bank.

  • Get the address: In Cash App → Bitcoin tab → "Deposit Bitcoin" → copy the address. Strike → Receive → Bitcoin.
  • Bonus: Strike also gives you a Lightning address (looks like you@strike.me) — perfect for tiny, near-free tips. Post that and the on-chain address.
  • Trade-off: requires ID (KYC), and "not your keys" — fine for a tip jar you cash out regularly.

Method 2 — A non-custodial mobile wallet (recommended for a tip jar) you own it

Download a free wallet app — Muun, Blue Wallet, or Phoenix — it generates a wallet on your phone. No ID, no account. You control the coins.

  • Get the address: Open the app → "Receive" → copy the Bitcoin address (and it'll also show a Lightning option).
  • Critical one-time step: the app shows a 12–24 word recovery phrase. Write it on paper, store it offline. That phrase is the money — anyone with it can take the funds, and losing it loses the funds. Never type it into a website.
  • Why this for a tip jar: no KYC, no middleman, you keep the keys, and it's still a 5-minute setup. Best balance of simple + sovereign.

Method 3 — A hardware wallet (most secure, for when it adds up) ~$70

A Trezor or Ledger device keeps the keys on dedicated offline hardware. Overkill for receiving coffees, but the right home if the jar ever holds real money.

  • Pattern: generate a receive address on the device, post that. You can even keep a "watch-only" copy on your phone to see incoming tips without exposing the keys.
  • Trade-off: costs money and is more setup than a tip jar needs on day one. Start with Method 2, graduate to this later.

A privacy note for posting a public address: Bitcoin is a public ledger. A single static address on your site means anyone can see its total balance and history. For a tip jar that's a fine, accepted trade-off (most do exactly this). If you want a touch more privacy later, a tool like BTCPay Server or a wallet's "payment request" feature can generate a fresh address per visitor — but don't let that stop you from launching with one static address now.

Recommendation: install Muun or Blue Wallet (Method 2), back up the seed phrase on paper, copy the receive address, and paste it into SUPPORT.btc in src/support.jsx. Add the Strike Lightning address too if you want frictionless micro-tips. Done.
