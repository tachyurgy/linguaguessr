01

Why a catalog fixes what a tip jar can't

The single-game Patreon has a structural ceiling. The portfolio doesn't. Same effort, a different shape.

The Patreon memo is good — but it quietly assumes the patron keeps paying to support LinguaGuessr. That's a fragile premise. A player funds a free game out of a burst of delight; a month later the delight is spent, the game is the same game, and there's no fresh reason to stay subscribed. One game gives you one moment of goodwill and then asks you to live on its memory.

What you actually described — branching into other games — is the missing renewable resource. When the patron is funding the maker, every new game is a brand-new hit of "oh, they made another one, I'm glad I'm part of this." The thing they're subscribed to keeps producing. That's exactly the relationship Patreon is built to monetize, and it's the difference between a number that plateaus and one that compounds.

One-game tip jar

A bucket

One launch spike → a few coffees → silence. Patrons churn because nothing new arrives. The dev log promises a future the single game can't keep producing.

A maker's catalog

A flywheel the fix

Each new game = a new Show-HN spike pointing at the same Patreon. Games market the patronage; the patronage funds the next game. Every release renews the reason to stay.

The reframe in one line: stop selling "support LinguaGuessr." Start selling "fund the person who keeps making these weird little browser games" — and let LinguaGuessr be the proof-of-concept that you do, in fact, ship.

02

The umbrella brand — the one new decision

If patrons fund the maker, the maker needs a name that outlives any single game. This is the only real new infrastructure the reframe demands.

Right now you have two names in play: LinguaGuessr (a game) and Levelbrook (the B2B consulting brand — keep it walled off, see §08). Neither can be the studio. You need a third, anonymous, player-facing maker identity — a tiny indie label that every game shares, that the Patreon is named after, and that the "Support" link points to.

It does three jobs: it gives the Patreon a home that isn't tied to one title, it lets each new game inherit instant recognition ("oh, the same people who made LinguaGuessr"), and it stays fully inside the anonymity rule — a label is, by definition, not a personal name.

Candidate vibes (pick a direction, not a final word)

The genre is small, perceptual, hand-made, slightly strange. The name should feel like a tiny workshop, not a corporation. A few directions to react to:

…Guessr family
Lean into the format: a shared "—Guessr" suffix makes the catalog self-evidently one studio. The label could simply be the format's home (e.g. "the Guessr workshop"). Risk: ties the brand to one mechanic.
A workshop / lab name
Something like "a tiny games lab" — evokes one person tinkering. Warm, human, format-agnostic so you're free to make non-guessing games later.
An object/animal mark
A short, concrete, memorable noun (an animal, a small object) + "games." Easy logo, easy to say, carries no baggage, and reads as indie not enterprise.
A made-up word
A coined, ownable, domain-available token. Maximum brand control, zero collision — but starts with zero meaning, so the games have to give it meaning.

This is your call, not mine — it's a taste decision with your name on it (figuratively). The strategic requirement is only that it (a) isn't "LinguaGuessr," (b) isn't "Levelbrook," (c) is anonymous, and (d) is broad enough to cover games beyond guessing. Pick the vibe; I'll pressure-test domain/handle availability against the shortlist.

03

The house genre — why LinguaGuessr is a template, not a one-off

You didn't accidentally make a hit-shaped thing. LinguaGuessr is a formula you can run again. Naming the formula is what makes a studio repeatable.

Strip LinguaGuessr to its bones and you get a pattern that's almost suspiciously reusable:

🎯
One mechanic

Guess the X by Y

A single perceptual judgment — language by ear. No tutorial needed. The whole game fits in one sentence, which is also its marketing.

📅
One habit

A daily challenge

The Wordle move: one puzzle a day, everyone gets the same one, a streak to protect. This is the retention engine — the reason someone comes back without you shipping anything.

📤
One loop

A shareable score

A spoiler-free result card ("🟩🟩⬜ I got 4/5") that begs to be posted. Free distribution baked into the win screen — every player is a channel.

That triad — one mechanic · one daily habit · one shareable result — is your house style. Any domain where a human can make a snap perceptual call becomes a candidate game. The corpus changes; the skeleton doesn't. That's why the second game is dramatically cheaper than the first (see §05), and why a studio is realistic for one person.

The strategic gift: these games are cheap to make, cheap to host, and natively viral — thin or no backend, a static bundle on Cloudflare Pages, and a share card doing your marketing. It's the rare format where a solo maker's economics actually close.

04

A starter slate — concrete next games

Not a commitment, a menu. Each one runs the §03 formula and reuses the §05 engine. Ranked roughly by "reuses what you already have" × "has a built-in audience that overlaps your traffic."

GameThe callWhy it fitsReuse
AccentGuessr Which accent / dialect of this language? Same audio corpus, harder mode. Closest possible sibling — arguably a LinguaGuessr mode you spin out as its own title. ~95%
ScriptGuessr What writing system / language is this text? The visual cousin of LinguaGuessr. No audio pipeline at all — just rendered strings. Linguistics/typography crowd. ~80%
CodeGuessr Which programming language is this snippet? Aimed straight at your HN/Reddit traffic — devs love guessing-game self-tests and share them hard. Pure text, trivially sourced. ~80%
BirdGuessr Which bird is making this call? Audio engine reused 1:1. Massive, devoted niche (birders), open-licensed corpora (xeno-canto). Different audience = new top-of-funnel. ~70%

The top three (Accent, Script, Code) are nearly free to build on what exists and aim at audiences you already reach. The bottom three open new audiences (birders, geo people, music people) — each one a fresh spike at the same Patreon. A realistic cadence is one new title a quarter: frequent enough to keep patrons fed, slow enough to stay a joy.

The one trap: don't let "studio" become a content treadmill. The dev-log memo's warning still holds double here — the goal is a handful of beloved games shipped at a humane pace, not a churn quota. If a quarter is quiet, a good dev log about why the next one is taking longer keeps patrons happier than a rushed dud.

05

The shared engine — build the skeleton once

The reason a solo studio is feasible: game #2 is mostly game #1 with a different corpus. Factor the skeleton out and each new title is days, not months.

LinguaGuessr already contains nearly every reusable part. The move is to lift the game-agnostic pieces into a shared core that every future title imports, leaving each game to supply only its corpus and its scoring rule.

Build once, reuse forever

  • Daily-challenge engine — deterministic "puzzle of the day," streak tracking, local-storage state (you have engine.js).
  • Round/scoring loop — 5 rounds, guess → reveal → score, the whole flow shell.
  • Share-card generator — the spoiler-free emoji result. Your single most important growth asset.
  • Support modal + tier ladder — support.jsx, already written, now points at the studio Patreon, not one game's.
  • Analytics beacon — the Worker → Analytics Engine setup, shared across all games' domains.
  • The "Engineered/indie" design system — type, color, components.

Per-game, all that's left

  • The corpus (clips/snippets/images + a manifest) and its loader.
  • The one scoring rule (exact match? region distance? family tree?).
  • The reveal panel content (what you teach after each guess).
  • A name, a color accent, and a splash.

Structure it as a shared package + thin games. A small monorepo (or a published-private core lib) holding the engine, share card, support layer, and design system; each game a thin app that imports it and ships a corpus. Bonus: a fix to the streak logic or the share card improves every game at once. This is the leverage that makes the catalog one person's reasonable life instead of six jobs.

06

The portal & cross-promotion — games that sell each other

A catalog's hidden superpower: each game is a recruiting station for the others. Wire that, and your traffic compounds instead of resetting per launch.

  • A studio portal page — one URL listing every game with a one-line pitch and a play button. The Patreon, the dev log, and "made by [studio]" all live here. This is the home base the maker identity points to.
  • A "more games by us" rail on every game's end screen — right next to the Support strip. A player who just enjoyed LinguaGuessr is the single most likely person to try CodeGuessr. Catch them at peak delight.
  • One shared Support layer — every game's "Support" opens the same modal pointing at the same Patreon/BMC/BTC. Six games, one funnel. A patron acquired through BirdGuessr funds LinguaGuessr's next corpus and vice versa.
  • One dev log, all games — patrons follow the studio's output, so a quiet month on one game is covered by progress on another. The log never goes dark.

The upgraded flywheel: ship game N → it spikes on HN/Reddit → its end screen converts coffees/patrons and funnels players into games 1…N-1 → those games' end screens funnel back → the whole catalog's traffic and patron base ratchets up with every release. A one-game flywheel turns once per launch; a catalog's turns N times.

07

What changes in the money layer

Good news: almost nothing. The Patreon memo's tiers were already secretly written for a studio. You're just promoting the subtext to the headline.

Re-read the existing tiers and you'll notice they already lean on the catalog — "early access to every new game I ship," "more weird little games," "vote on the next game idea." The reframe doesn't rewrite them; it makes the catalog the spine instead of a perk buried at $15.

08

The wall with Levelbrook holds — and gets cleaner

Three identities now, not two. The separation the anonymity rule already demands does exactly the right thing here.

🏢
B2B · real money

Levelbrook

The senior Rails consulting practice. Five-figure engagements, "Hire us," consulting.levelbrook.com. Untouched by any of this.

🎮
Player-facing · indie

The studio

The new anonymous maker label. Owns the games, the Patreon, the dev log. Warm, first-person, format-agnostic.

🎧
A product

LinguaGuessr (et al.)

Individual titles under the studio. Each has its own name/accent but shares the engine, the support funnel, and the maker brand.

LinguaGuessr currently sits on a Levelbrook subdomain (lingua.levelbrook.com) and wears a "demo by Levelbrook" hat for the consulting funnel. That's fine to keep — but as the catalog grows, the games increasingly want to live under the studio's own domain, with the "this maker also consults as Levelbrook" link being the quiet bridge, not the frame. The consulting funnel still benefits ("look, real shipped products with paying fans") without the indie voice being subordinate to the suit.

Open decision for you: do the games eventually migrate to a standalone studio domain, or stay as Levelbrook subdomains? Not urgent — LinguaGuessr stays put for now — but worth deciding before game #3, because it sets where the cross-promo portal lives and what the share cards say.

09

Do this next

The reframe costs almost no new building — it's mostly a naming decision and a refactor you'd want anyway.

  1. Pick the studio vibe (§02). Tell me a direction; I'll check domains + Patreon/BMC/social handle availability against a shortlist.
  2. Re-point the Patreon from "LinguaGuessr" to the studio. Same four tiers; promote "every new game first" to the headline; rewrite the micro-story as the maker's mission.
  3. Factor out the shared core (§05) — lift engine, share card, support modal, analytics, and design system into one importable package. Do this with LinguaGuessr so game #2 is days of work.
  4. Build game #2 from the top of the slate (AccentGuessr or CodeGuessr — highest reuse, audience you already reach). Ship it as the proof the studio is real.
  5. Stand up the portal + cross-promo rail (§06) so the two games start funneling into each other and into one Patreon.
  6. Relaunch as a studio — "Show HN: I made a second guessing game" lands the catalog story, and every spike now hits a funnel built to compound.

The honest summary: you already did the hard part — you proved you can ship a beloved, viral, hand-made browser game. The Patreon memo just priced that proof too narrowly. Sell the habit of shipping them, not the one you've shipped, and the money layer finally has something worth subscribing to.
